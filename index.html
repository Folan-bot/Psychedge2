Content — Obedience

Theories of Obedience

Obedience is a form of social influence where an individual acts in response to a direct order from a perceived authority figure. Edexcel requires two theories: Agency Theory and Social Power Theory.

Agency Theory (Milgram, 1974)

Core Definition
Agency theory proposes that people shift between two states: the autonomous state (acting on their own conscience) and the agentic state (acting as an agent of authority). In the agentic state, individuals transfer moral responsibility upward, making obedience easier to sustain.

The agentic shift is the transition between states — triggered by entering a social hierarchy with a legitimate authority. Once in the agentic state, binding factors (politeness, gradual escalation, fear of consequences) keep the person obedient even under moral strain.

💡 Exam Tip
When applying agency theory to a scenario, always: (1) name the theory, (2) identify the agentic shift, (3) name a specific binding factor, and (4) explain why moral responsibility is transferred. Four steps = 4 marks covered.

Social Power Theory (French & Raven, 1959)

Core Definition
French and Raven identified five bases of power that authority figures use to elicit obedience: Reward, Coercive, Legitimate, Referent, and Expert (RCLRE).
Reward Power
Compliance via reward
Obedience because the authority can provide something desirable — pay, praise, or promotion.
Coercive Power
Compliance via threat
Obedience driven by fear of punishment or negative consequences for non-compliance.
Legitimate Power
Compliance via role
The authority holds a recognised social role (doctor, experimenter, police). Their right to command is accepted as valid.
Referent Power
Compliance via identity
Obedience because the individual identifies with or admires the authority figure.
Expert Power
Compliance via knowledge
The authority is perceived to have superior knowledge. Milgram's experimenter in a lab coat used this directly.
💡 Mnemonic — RCLRE
"Really Cool Lecturers Receive Exams" = Reward, Coercive, Legitimate, Referent, Expert. Common 2-mark question: name two bases of social power.

Content — Conformity

Types & Explanations of Conformity

Conformity is a change in behaviour or belief due to real or imagined group pressure. Edexcel requires Kelman's three types and the two explanations: NSI and ISI.

Kelman's Three Types

Deepest
Internalisation
Genuine, permanent change to both public behaviour and private beliefs. Persists without group. Associated with ISI.
Intermediate
Identification
Change to public and private beliefs while the individual values group membership. E.g. new professional role.
Most superficial
Compliance
Only public behaviour changes. Private beliefs unchanged. Stops when group pressure lifts. Associated with NSI.
NSI vs ISI
Normative Social Influence (NSI): We conform to gain approval and avoid rejection — leads to compliance.

Informational Social Influence (ISI): We conform because we believe others have better information (ambiguous situations) — leads to internalisation.
📋 Examiner Report
Students frequently confuse ISI and NSI, or fail to link them to the correct type of conformity. A strong answer always states: ISI → internalisation (accept the belief); NSI → compliance (only change behaviour). Missing this link loses evaluation marks.

Minority Influence (Moscovici, 1976)

Factor 1
Consistency
Minority maintains position over time (diachronic) and across situations (synchronic). Signals confidence, makes majority reconsider.
Factor 2
Commitment
Minority demonstrates investment, often at personal cost (augmentation principle). Increases perceived sincerity.
Factor 3
Flexibility
Some willingness to adapt makes the minority more persuasive. Rigid minorities appear dogmatic and are dismissed.
💡 Exam Tip
Minority influence tends to produce conversion — private, internalised change via indirect/effortful processing. Always contrast with majority influence (compliance, direct route) when answering 'how does minority influence work' questions.

Key Studies

Core Studies

All five studies are examinable. Moscovici is the named classic study; Burger and Haun are the named contemporary studies. Milgram and Asch underpin all obedience and conformity content.

Classic Study — Obedience
Milgram (1963) — Behavioural Study of Obedience
+
Aim
To investigate whether ordinary Americans would obey authority instructions to harm an innocent person.
Sample
40 male participants, aged 20–50. Volunteer sample via newspaper ad. Confederate acted as 'learner'.
Procedure
Participants administered what they believed were electric shocks (15–450V, in 15V increments) for wrong answers. Experimenter gave standardised prods. Learner's protests were pre-recorded.
Results
65% (26/40) administered 450V. All continued to at least 300V. Psychiatrists had predicted fewer than 1% would reach maximum.
Conclusion
Situational factors (authority, location, legitimacy) override individual conscience. Ordinary people can cause harm in the agentic state under a legitimate authority structure.

Variation Studies (Spec-Required)

VariationChangeRateConclusion

Evaluation

TypePointElaboration
✓ StrengthHigh internal validityStandardised procedure and scripted prods allow replication. Burger (2009) closely followed the design.
✓ StrengthReal behaviour observedVisible distress (trembling, sweating) confirms participants believed the situation — obedience was genuine.
✗ WeaknessSerious ethical violationsDeception, psychological harm, and prods that undermined the right to withdraw — all breach BPS guidelines.
✗ WeaknessLow ecological validityOrne & Holland (1968): demand characteristics may have inflated obedience. Artificial setting limits real-world generalisation.
✗ WeaknessAndrocentric sampleAll-male, all-American sample limits cross-gender and cross-cultural generalisability.
📋 Examiner Report
Students lose marks by not being precise. Examiners want: the exact shock range (15–450V), the word 'prods' (not just 'orders'), and the confederate as 'learner'. On ethics, always link the violation to the specific BPS principle — not just 'it was unethical'.
Core Study — Conformity
Asch (1951) — Studies of Independence and Conformity
+
Aim
To investigate whether individuals would conform to a clearly incorrect unanimous majority in an unambiguous perceptual task.
Sample
123 male US undergraduates, placed with 6–8 confederates each.
Procedure
Participants matched a standard line to one of three comparison lines. In 12 of 18 critical trials, confederates gave an obviously wrong unanimous answer.
Results
36.8% conformity rate on critical trials. 75% conformed at least once. 25% never conformed. Control condition error rate: <1%.
Conclusion
Conformity occurs even when the correct answer is obvious — driven by NSI (desire to avoid rejection), not ISI.
📋 Examiner Report
Very common error: stating that 36.8% "conformed on all trials." This is the per-trial rate, not a total. Also: Asch's participants knew the answers were wrong — this confirms NSI, not ISI. Make this explicit in your answer.
Classic Study — Minority Influence
Moscovici et al. (1969) — Influence of a Consistent Minority on Colour Perception
+
Aim
To investigate whether a consistent minority could influence a majority's perception in a colour-naming task.
Sample
172 female participants with confirmed normal colour vision. Groups of 6: 4 naive, 2 confederates.
Procedure
36 clearly blue slides shown (varying luminance). Confederates consistently called slides 'green' (consistent condition) or alternated 'green'/'blue' (inconsistent condition).
Results
Consistent minority: 8.42% 'green' responses (vs 1.25% inconsistent, 0.25% control). 32% of consistent condition participants called at least one slide 'green'.
Conclusion
Consistent minorities can influence majorities — but only through consistency. Behavioural style is the key mechanism of minority influence.
💡 Evaluation Tip
Strength: high internal validity (controlled, screened sample). Weakness: artificial colour-naming task lacks ecological validity — hard to generalise to real political/social minority influence. Use this tension for a nuanced AO3 paragraph.
Contemporary Study — Obedience
Burger (2009) — Replicating Milgram: Would People Still Obey Today?
+
Aim
To ethically replicate Milgram and test whether obedience rates have changed since the 1960s.
Sample
70 participants (29M, 41F), aged 20–81. Pre-screened to exclude psychological conditions.
Procedure
Modified Milgram — stopped at 150V (ethical cut-off). Participants told they could stop at this point. Included a 'modelled refusal' condition (confederate refused to continue).
Results
70% continued to 150V (cf. Milgram's 82.5% at same point). Modelled refusal condition: 52.5% continued.
Conclusion
Obedience to authority is largely unchanged since 1963. Situational factors identified by Milgram remain powerful.
📋 Examiner Report
Students often confuse Burger's 70% (at 150V) with Milgram's 65% (at 450V). These are not directly comparable. The ethical modification (stopping at 150V) is both a strength (protects participants) and a weakness (limits direct comparison). State both sides.
Contemporary Study — Conformity
Haun et al. (2014) — Conformity to Peer Opinions Lasts No More Than 3 Days
+
Aim
To investigate whether children's conformity to peer group opinions was lasting or temporary.
Sample
126 children (~age 8) from a German school. Tested individually and in peer groups.
Procedure
Children identified which of two objects appeared most often in animated scenes. After hearing the incorrect majority peer answer, responses were tested again after 1 day and 1 week.
Results
~22% conformed to incorrect peer answers. After 1 day, children reverted toward original answers. After 1 week, conformity effects were negligible.
Conclusion
Peer conformity in children is NSI-driven compliance, not internalisation — it rapidly disappears without sustained group pressure.
💡 Linking to Kelman
Haun et al. directly supports the compliance/internalisation distinction. The rapid fade of conformity confirms that children's conformity was surface-level (NSI/compliance), not a genuine belief change. Use this to evaluate Kelman's types in extended writing questions.
Methods

Research Methods in Social Psychology

Edexcel requires knowledge of self-report methods, sampling, quantitative data analysis, and BPS ethics — all contextualised to social psychology research.

Self-Report Methods

Questionnaires
Written questions completed without researcher input. Include open (qualitative), closed (quantitative), and ranked scale questions (e.g. Likert).

Strengths: Large samples; cost-effective; no interviewer bias; anonymity reduces social desirability.
Weaknesses: Social desirability bias; response sets; cannot probe; misunderstanding of questions.
Interviews
Structured: Identical questions, identical order. High reliability, easy to compare, low validity.

Semi-structured: Core questions + probing. Balance of reliability and validity.

Unstructured: Guided by participant. Highest validity and depth. Low reliability; strong interviewer effects.
💡 Researcher Effects
The spec explicitly mentions 'researcher effects' in self-report contexts. This refers to how the interviewer's characteristics (age, gender, manner) influence participant responses — reducing validity. Always contrast with questionnaires, which remove researcher presence.

Sampling Techniques

MethodHow it worksStrengthWeakness
RandomEqual chance of selection for all members of target population.Minimises sampling bias; most representative.Requires full population list; refusals reduce randomness.
StratifiedPopulation divided into subgroups; proportional random selection from each.Captures subgroup variation; proportionally representative.Complex; strata must be clearly defined.
VolunteerParticipants self-select via advertisement — e.g. Milgram's newspaper ad.Easy and ethical; participants are willing.Volunteer bias — may attract a particular type of person (e.g. more compliant).

Quantitative Data Analysis

Measures of Central Tendency
Mean: Sum ÷ n. Sensitive to outliers — avoid with skewed data.

Median: Middle value when ranked. Unaffected by extremes — better for skewed distributions.

Mode: Most frequent value. Only measure usable with nominal/categorical data.
Measures of Dispersion
Range: Highest − Lowest (+1). Simple but sensitive to outliers.

Standard Deviation: Average distance of each score from the mean. Higher SD = greater spread. More informative than range — uses all data points.

BPS Ethical Guidelines

Guideline 1
Informed Consent
Participants must have sufficient info to decide to take part. Milgram's deception violated this — justified only by debriefing.
Guideline 2
Right to Withdraw
Participants must be able to leave at any time. Milgram's prods ("You have no other choice") directly violated this principle.
Guideline 3
Protection from Harm
Researchers must protect participants from physical and psychological harm. Milgram's participants showed extreme visible distress.
Guideline 4
Debrief
Participants must be fully informed post-study. Milgram did debrief and conducted a 1-year follow-up.
Guideline 5
Confidentiality
Personal data must be kept private and not published without consent. Anonymity must be maintained where promised.
Guideline 6
Risk Management
BPS (2009) requires researchers to assess, minimise, and monitor risks before, during, and after research — especially with vulnerable participants.
Exam Skills

Worked Answers & Examiner Tips

Fully worked sample answers for the three main question types in Edexcel Social Psychology, annotated with AO1/AO2/AO3 and real examiner report insights.

4-Mark Question — Describe

Worked Answer — Agency Theory4 marks · AO1 only
"Describe agency theory as an explanation of obedience." [4 marks]
AO1Name and define: Agency theory (Milgram, 1974) proposes that people operate in one of two states: the autonomous state (acting on their own conscience) or the agentic state (acting as an agent of an authority figure).
AO1Explain the agentic shift: The transition between states — the agentic shift — occurs when an individual enters a hierarchical structure and recognises a legitimate authority above them.
AO1Binding factors and moral strain: In the agentic state, responsibility is transferred upward. Even when experiencing moral strain, binding factors (such as politeness, gradual commitment, and fear of consequences) keep the individual obedient.
AO1 × 3 → 4 marksNamed theorist, both states defined, shift explained, binding factors included.
📋 Examiner Report
Many students describe the agentic state but omit the autonomous state — losing the contrast that makes the theory meaningful. The best answers name 'binding factors' specifically (not just 'social pressure') and always include 'moral strain' to show understanding of the internal conflict involved.

8-Mark Question — Outline & Evaluate

Worked Answer — Kelman's Types8 marks · AO1 + AO3
"Outline and evaluate Kelman's types of conformity." [8 marks]
AO1Define all three types: Kelman (1958) identified three types. Compliance: only public behaviour changes, stops when group pressure lifts. Identification: public and private changes while group membership is valued. Internalisation: permanent change to both behaviour and belief, persists without the group.
AO1Link to ISI/NSI: Compliance is driven by NSI (desire for approval); internalisation arises from ISI (accepting others' superior information), especially in ambiguous situations.
AO3Supporting evidence: Asch (1951) supports the compliance/internalisation distinction — when answers were given in writing (anonymously), conformity fell to near zero. This shows that conformity in the standard condition was compliance driven by NSI, not genuine belief change.
AO3Contradictory evidence: Haun et al. (2014) found children's peer conformity lasted no more than 3 days, reverting to original answers. This challenges whether internalisation commonly occurs from everyday social influence, suggesting most conformity may be short-lived compliance.
AO3Methodological limitation: The three types are conceptually distinct but difficult to measure — self-reports of private beliefs are prone to social desirability bias, making it hard to verify whether genuine internalisation has occurred or whether participants are simply reporting expected answers.
AO1 × 4 + AO3 × 4 = 8 marks3 developed AO3 points; each with evidence or elaboration. Band 4 response.
💡 8-Mark Formula
2 AO1 paragraphs + 3 AO3 points (each: make point → develop → evidence/example). Marks split 4/4 — don't write excess AO1. Examiners reward developed evaluation over quantity of points.

12-Mark Question — Discuss (Extended Writing)

Worked Answer — Obedience Research12 marks · AO1 + AO2 + AO3
"Discuss research into obedience, including at least one study." [12 marks]
AO1Outline key research: Milgram (1963) found 65% of 40 male volunteers administered what they believed was a maximum 450V shock when instructed by an authority figure — far exceeding psychiatric predictions of <1%. All participants continued to at least 300V.
AO1Link to theory: Agency theory explains this via the agentic shift — participants transferred moral responsibility to the experimenter. Variation studies confirm: telephonic instructions (Exp. 7) reduced obedience to 20.5%, showing physical presence of authority is a key binding factor.
AO2Real-world application: Milgram's findings help explain the My Lai massacre (1968), where US soldiers obeyed orders to harm civilians despite visible moral strain. The military hierarchy produced the conditions for an agentic state — consistent with agency theory's predictions.
AO3Supporting replication: Burger (2009) found 70% of participants continued to 150V — near-identical to Milgram's 82.5% at that point. This cross-temporal consistency strengthens the validity of Milgram's findings, suggesting situational determinants of obedience are stable across decades.
AO3Ethical critique: However, Milgram violated multiple BPS principles: deception (informed consent), prods preventing withdrawal (right to withdraw), and psychological harm (protection from harm). Baumrind (1964) argued this could not be justified by the research value. Milgram's 1-year follow-up is insufficient mitigation.
AO3Ecological validity: Orne & Holland (1968) argued that demand characteristics may have inflated obedience — participants may have suspected the shocks were fake given the laboratory setting. If so, this challenges whether genuine obedience to authority was being measured at all.
AO1 × 4 + AO2 × 2 + AO3 × 6 = 12 marksReal-world AO2, 3 developed AO3 points, structured AO1. Band 4 response.
📋 Examiner Report — 12-Mark Questions
Students fall into Band 2 (5–8 marks) by writing too much AO1 and too little AO3. The AO2 marks require real-world application — if no scenario is given, provide your own (e.g. My Lai, Nuremberg, workplace settings). Strong AO3 includes a developed counter-argument, not just a list of weaknesses. Each evaluation point must be explained and evidenced, not just stated.
