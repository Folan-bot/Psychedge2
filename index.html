Content — Obedience

Theories of Obedience

Obedience is a form of social influence where an individual follows a direct order from an authority figure. Edexcel requires two theories: Agency Theory and Social Power Theory.

Agency Theory (Milgram, 1974)

Core Definition
Agency theory proposes that people shift between the autonomous state (acting on their own conscience) and the agentic state (acting as an agent of authority). In the agentic state, moral responsibility is transferred upward, making obedience easier to sustain.

The agentic shift is the transition between states, triggered by entering a social hierarchy with a legitimate authority. Binding factors (politeness, gradual escalation, fear of consequences) keep the individual obedient even under moral strain.

💡 Exam Tip
When applying agency theory to a scenario: (1) name the theory, (2) identify the agentic shift, (3) name a binding factor, (4) explain why responsibility is transferred. Four steps = 4 marks covered.

Social Power Theory (French & Raven, 1959)

Core Definition
French and Raven identified five bases of power: Reward, Coercive, Legitimate, Referent, and Expert (RCLRE).
Reward Power
Compliance via reward
Authority can provide something desirable — pay, praise, or promotion.
Coercive Power
Compliance via threat
Obedience driven by fear of punishment for non-compliance.
Legitimate Power
Compliance via role
Authority holds a recognised social role whose right to command is accepted as valid.
Referent Power
Compliance via identity
Individual identifies with or admires the authority figure.
Expert Power
Compliance via knowledge
Authority perceived to have superior knowledge — Milgram's experimenter's lab coat.
💡 Mnemonic — RCLRE
"Really Cool Lecturers Receive Exams" = Reward, Coercive, Legitimate, Referent, Expert. Common 2-marker asks you to name two bases.

Content — Conformity

Types & Explanations of Conformity

Conformity is a change in behaviour or belief due to real or imagined group pressure. Edexcel requires Kelman's three types and NSI/ISI explanations.

Kelman's Three Types

Deepest
Internalisation
Permanent change to both public behaviour and private beliefs. Persists without the group. Associated with ISI.
Intermediate
Identification
Change while individual values group membership. E.g. adopting a new professional role's norms.
Most superficial
Compliance
Only public behaviour changes. Stops when group pressure lifts. Associated with NSI.
NSI vs ISI
Normative Social Influence (NSI): Conform to gain approval, avoid rejection → compliance.

Informational Social Influence (ISI): Conform because others have better information → internalisation.
📋 Examiner Report
Students frequently fail to link ISI → internalisation and NSI → compliance. Always state this chain explicitly.

Minority Influence (Moscovici, 1976)

Factor 1
Consistency
Maintains position across time (diachronic) and situations (synchronic). Signals confidence.
Factor 2
Commitment
Investment at personal cost (augmentation principle). Increases perceived sincerity.
Factor 3
Flexibility
Some willingness to adapt. Rigid minorities appear dogmatic and are dismissed.

Factors Affecting Conformity

Key Studies

Core Studies

All five studies are examinable. Moscovici is the named classic study; Burger and Haun are named contemporary studies.

Classic Study — Obedience
Milgram (1963) — Behavioural Study of Obedience
+
Aim
To investigate whether ordinary Americans would obey authority instructions to harm an innocent person.
Sample
40 male participants, aged 20–50. Volunteer sample via newspaper ad. Confederate acted as 'learner'.
Procedure
Participants administered believed electric shocks (15–450V, 15V increments) for wrong answers. Experimenter gave standardised prods. Learner's protests pre-recorded.
Results
65% (26/40) administered 450V. All continued to at least 300V. Psychiatrists predicted fewer than 1% would reach maximum.
Conclusion
Situational factors override individual conscience. Ordinary people cause harm in the agentic state under a legitimate authority structure.

Variation Studies

VariationChangeRateConclusion
Exp. 7Instructions by phone20.5%Physical presence increases obedience
Exp. 10Run-down Bridgeport office47.5%Prestige signals legitimate power
Exp. 13Ordinary man gives orders20%Legitimate role/uniform essential

Evaluation

TypePointElaboration
✓ StrengthHigh internal validityStandardised procedure and scripted prods allow replication — Burger (2009) closely followed the design.
✓ StrengthReal behaviour observedVisible distress confirms participants believed the situation — obedience was genuine.
✗ WeaknessEthical violationsDeception, psychological harm, and prods undermining right to withdraw — all breach BPS guidelines.
✗ WeaknessEcological validityOrne & Holland (1968): demand characteristics may have inflated obedience. Artificial lab setting.
📋 Examiner Report
Always specify the shock range (15–450V), use the word 'prods', and call the confederate a 'learner'. Link each ethical violation to a specific BPS principle — not just 'it was unethical'.
Core Study — Conformity
Asch (1951) — Studies of Independence and Conformity
+
Aim
To investigate whether individuals would conform to a clearly incorrect unanimous majority.
Sample
123 male US undergraduates placed with 6–8 confederates each.
Procedure
Participants matched a standard line to one of three comparison lines. In 12 of 18 critical trials, confederates gave an obviously wrong unanimous answer.
Results
36.8% conformity rate per critical trial. 75% conformed at least once. Control condition error rate: <1%.
Conclusion
Conformity occurs even when the answer is obvious — driven by NSI, not ISI.
📋 Examiner Report
Common error: stating 36.8% "conformed on all trials" — this is the per-trial rate. Participants knew the answers were wrong, confirming NSI not ISI. State this explicitly.
Classic Study — Minority Influence
Moscovici et al. (1969) — Influence of a Consistent Minority on Colour Perception
+
Aim
To investigate whether a consistent minority could influence the majority's responses in a colour-naming task.
Sample
172 female participants with normal colour vision. Groups of 6: 4 naive, 2 confederates.
Procedure
36 clearly blue slides shown. Confederates consistently called slides 'green' (consistent) or alternated (inconsistent condition).
Results
Consistent minority: 8.42% 'green' responses vs 1.25% inconsistent and 0.25% control. 32% of consistent condition participants called at least one slide 'green'.
Conclusion
Consistent minorities can influence majorities. Behavioural consistency is the key mechanism of minority influence.
Contemporary Study — Obedience
Burger (2009) — Replicating Milgram: Would People Still Obey Today?
+
Aim
To ethically replicate Milgram and test whether obedience rates have changed since the 1960s.
Sample
70 participants (29M, 41F), aged 20–81. Pre-screened to exclude psychological conditions.
Procedure
Modified Milgram — stopped at 150V. Participants told they could stop. Modelled refusal condition included.
Results
70% continued to 150V. Modelled refusal condition: 52.5%.
Conclusion
Obedience to authority is largely unchanged since 1963.
📋 Examiner Report
Don't confuse Burger's 70% (at 150V) with Milgram's 65% (at 450V) — they are not directly comparable. State both the ethical strength and the limitation of stopping early.
Contemporary Study — Conformity
Haun et al. (2014) — Conformity to Peer Opinions Lasts No More Than 3 Days
+
Aim
To investigate whether children's conformity to peer opinions was lasting or temporary.
Sample
126 children (~age 8) from a German school.
Procedure
Children identified which object appeared most in scenes. After hearing incorrect peer majority answers, responses tested after 1 day and 1 week.
Results
~22% conformed initially. After 1 week, conformity effects negligible.
Conclusion
Peer conformity in children is NSI-driven compliance, not internalisation — dissipates without sustained group pressure.
💡 Link to Kelman
Rapid fade of conformity confirms NSI/compliance, not genuine belief change. Use this to evaluate Kelman's types in extended writing.
Methods

Research Methods in Social Psychology

Edexcel requires knowledge of self-report methods, sampling, quantitative data analysis, and BPS ethics.

Self-Report Methods

Questionnaires
Written questions without researcher input. Include open (qualitative), closed (quantitative), and ranked scale questions.

Strengths: Large samples; cost-effective; no interviewer bias.
Weaknesses: Social desirability bias; cannot probe answers.
Interviews
Structured: Identical questions — high reliability, low validity.
Semi-structured: Core questions + probing — balanced.
Unstructured: Participant-led — highest validity, lowest reliability. Strong interviewer effects.
💡 Researcher Effects
The spec explicitly mentions 'researcher effects' — how the interviewer's characteristics influence participant responses, reducing validity. Always contrast with questionnaires where researcher presence is removed.

Sampling Techniques

MethodHow it worksStrengthWeakness
RandomEqual chance for all members of target population.Minimises bias; most representative.Requires full population list.
StratifiedProportional random selection from subgroups.Captures subgroup variation.Complex; strata must be defined.
VolunteerParticipants self-select via advertisement.Easy and ethical; willing participants.Volunteer bias — may attract compliant individuals.
OpportunityMost available individuals selected.Quick and practical.Highly unrepresentative.

BPS Ethical Guidelines

Guideline 1
Informed Consent
Sufficient info to decide to participate. Milgram's deception violated this.
Guideline 2
Right to Withdraw
Must be able to leave at any time. Milgram's prods directly violated this.
Guideline 3
Protection from Harm
Protect from physical and psychological harm. Milgram's participants showed extreme distress.
Guideline 4
Debrief
Fully informed post-study. Milgram debriefed and followed up after 1 year.
Guideline 5
Confidentiality
Personal data kept private; anonymity maintained where promised.
Guideline 6
Risk Management
BPS (2009): assess, minimise, and monitor risks before, during, and after research.

Fully worked sample answers for the three main question types, annotated with AO1/AO2/AO3.

4-Mark — Describe

Agency Theory4 marks · AO1
"Describe agency theory as an explanation of obedience." [4 marks]
AO1Define: Agency theory (Milgram, 1974) proposes that people operate in either the autonomous state or the agentic state (acting as an agent of authority).
AO1Agentic shift: The transition occurs when an individual enters a hierarchical structure and recognises a legitimate authority above them.
AO1Binding factors: In the agentic state, responsibility transfers upward. Even under moral strain, binding factors such as politeness and gradual commitment keep the individual obedient.
AO1 × 3 → 4 marksNamed theorist, both states, shift, binding factors.

8-Mark — Outline & Evaluate

Kelman's Types of Conformity8 marks · AO1 + AO3
"Outline and evaluate Kelman's types of conformity." [8 marks]
AO1Three types: Kelman (1958) identified compliance (public only, stops with pressure), identification (public and private while membership is valued), and internalisation (permanent, persists without the group).
AO1Link to ISI/NSI: Compliance is driven by NSI (desire for approval); internalisation arises from ISI (accepting others' information as accurate).
AO3Supporting evidence: Asch (1951) — when answers were given anonymously, conformity fell to ~0%, showing the standard condition was NSI-driven compliance, not genuine belief change.
AO3Contradictory evidence: Haun et al. (2014) found children's peer conformity lasted no more than 3 days, challenging whether internalisation commonly occurs from everyday social influence.
AO3Methodological limitation: Self-reports of private beliefs are prone to social desirability bias, making it hard to verify whether genuine internalisation occurred.
AO1 × 4 + AO3 × 4 = 8 marks3 developed AO3 points with evidence. Band 4.
💡 8-Mark Formula
2 AO1 paragraphs + 3 AO3 points. Marks split 4/4. Don't overload AO1. Each AO3 point: make it → develop it → evidence it.

Content — Memory Models

The Multi-Store Model (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968)

The multi-store model (MSM) proposes that memory consists of three separate stores: the sensory register, short-term memory, and long-term memory — each with distinct characteristics and processes.

Core Model
Information flows from the environment into the sensory register, then via attention into short-term memory (STM), then via rehearsal into long-term memory (LTM). Each store differs in capacity, duration, and encoding.
Sensory Register
First store
Capacity: Very large (all sensory input).
Duration: Very brief (¼–½ second).
Encoding: Modality-specific (visual, auditory etc.).
Information is lost through decay unless attended to.
Short-Term Memory
Working store
Capacity: 7 ± 2 chunks (Miller, 1956).
Duration: 15–30 seconds without rehearsal.
Encoding: Primarily acoustic (Conrad, 1964).
Lost through displacement or decay.
Long-Term Memory
Permanent store
Capacity: Unlimited.
Duration: Potentially lifetime.
Encoding: Primarily semantic (meaning-based).
Includes episodic, semantic, and procedural memory.

Evaluation of the MSM

TypePointElaboration
✓ StrengthSupporting case study evidencePatient HM (Milner, 1957) had his hippocampus removed and could form no new LTMs but retained intact STM — supporting the distinction between stores.
✓ StrengthSerial position effectResearch shows better recall for first (primacy — LTM) and last (recency — STM) items in a list, consistent with two separate stores.
✗ WeaknessOversimplifies STMShallice & Warrington (1970): patient KF had impaired verbal STM but intact visual STM — suggesting STM is not a single unitary store.
✗ WeaknessPassive rehearsal modelCraik & Lockhart (1972): depth of processing (not just rehearsal) determines LTM — the MSM overemphasises maintenance rehearsal.

The Working Memory Model (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974)

The WMM replaced the unitary STM store with a multi-component system that actively processes and manipulates information, not just holds it passively.

Component 1
Central Executive
The 'boss' — allocates attention and coordinates the slave systems. Has limited capacity. Does not store information directly. Responsible for planning, decision-making, and switching attention.
Component 2
Phonological Loop
Handles verbal/auditory information. Split into the phonological store (inner ear — holds sounds for ~2 seconds) and articulatory control process (inner voice — rehearses sounds). Explains the word length effect.
Component 3
Visuo-spatial Sketchpad
The 'inner eye' — processes and stores visual and spatial information. Has limited capacity. Baddeley (1975): glass half full task shows it is separate from the phonological loop.
Component 4
Episodic Buffer
Added by Baddeley (2000). A temporary store linking information from the slave systems and LTM into a single episode. Explains how we combine information from multiple sources coherently.
💡 Exam Tip — Dual Task Studies
A key strength of the WMM is support from dual-task studies: performing two tasks using the same component leads to interference (e.g. two visual tasks), but two tasks using different components do not. This provides direct evidence for the separate slave systems. Always mention this when evaluating the WMM.
📋 Examiner Report
Students frequently confuse the phonological store with the articulatory control process, or omit the episodic buffer entirely. The best answers describe all four components with their specific functions. The central executive is often described vaguely as 'controlling' — be precise: it allocates attentional resources.

Reconstructive Memory (Bartlett, 1932)

Bartlett proposed that memory is not a passive recording but an active, reconstructive process influenced by existing knowledge, expectations, and cultural schemas.

Schema Theory
A schema is a mental framework of existing knowledge and expectations built up through experience. When encoding or retrieving memories, we use schemas to fill in gaps, making memory prone to distortion — particularly for unfamiliar or schema-inconsistent material.

Bartlett's classic study used the Native American folk tale The War of the Ghosts. British participants repeatedly reproduced the story, and Bartlett found that they systematically rationalised unfamiliar elements (e.g. canoes became boats), omitted schema-inconsistent details, and distorted information to fit their own cultural expectations.

This suggests that LTM is reconstructive rather than reproductive — we do not recall a perfect copy but actively rebuild memories using schemas at the point of retrieval.

💡 Real-World Application — EWT
Reconstructive memory has major implications for eyewitness testimony (EWT). If memory is schema-driven, witnesses may unconsciously fill gaps with expectations rather than facts — particularly under stress or when identifying faces from a different ethnic group (cross-race effect). Loftus & Palmer's (1974) post-event misinformation research directly supports this.
Error Type 1
Rationalisation
Schema-inconsistent elements are changed to fit existing knowledge. E.g. 'canoe' → 'boat'.
Error Type 2
Omission
Details that don't fit the schema are dropped from recall entirely.
Error Type 3
Distortion
Details are altered to make the memory more coherent or culturally familiar.
Error Type 4
Confabulation
Details are added that were never there — invented to fill gaps and create a plausible narrative.
Key Studies

Core Studies

The three studies below are named in the Edexcel spec. Bartlett is the classic study; Schmolck and Sacchi are the contemporary studies.

Classic Study — Reconstructive Memory
Bartlett (1932) — Remembering: A Study in Experimental and Social Psychology
+
Aim
To investigate how memory is affected by prior knowledge and cultural schemas when recalling an unfamiliar story.
Sample
British participants; number not precisely specified. Used serial reproduction (same participants recalled repeatedly) and repeated reproduction methods.
Procedure
Participants read 'The War of the Ghosts' — a Native American folk tale with unfamiliar cultural themes. They were asked to recall it after various intervals (from 15 minutes to several years).
Results
Recalls became progressively shorter and more distorted. Cultural rationalisations were common (e.g. 'canoes' → 'boats'). Schema-inconsistent supernatural elements were omitted or made more familiar. Stories became more coherent but less accurate.
Conclusion
Memory is reconstructive, not reproductive — it is influenced by prior knowledge (schemas). We distort memories to fit our existing understanding of the world.

Evaluation

TypePointElaboration
✓ StrengthReal-world applicationFindings directly apply to EWT reliability and clinical interviews. Understanding schema-driven distortion has influenced how police gather witness statements.
✓ StrengthEcological validityRecalled a meaningful narrative rather than meaningless syllables — more like real-life memory use than lab-based word list studies.
✗ WeaknessLack of experimental controlNo standardised procedure; varied time intervals; no control group. Hard to establish cause and effect or replicate precisely.
✗ WeaknessDemand characteristicsParticipants knew they were being tested on memory — may have guessed the aim and altered behaviour accordingly, reducing validity.
📋 Examiner Report
Students often describe Bartlett's findings correctly but then fail to link them to schema theory in evaluation. The key claim is not just that memories change — it's that they change in systematic, schema-consistent ways. This specificity is what earns the higher AO3 marks.
Contemporary Study — Long-Term Memory
Schmolck et al. (2002) — Semantic Knowledge in Patient HM and Organic Amnesia
+
Aim
To investigate the role of the hippocampus in semantic and episodic memory by studying patients with hippocampal damage.
Sample
Patient HM (bilateral hippocampal removal) and a control group of patients with varying degrees of hippocampal damage assessed via MRI.
Procedure
Semantic memory tested using picture naming tasks, category naming, and vocabulary tests. Episodic memory tested using recall of personal events. Damage extent quantified via MRI.
Results
HM showed severely impaired episodic memory but surprisingly intact semantic knowledge for material learned before surgery. Patients with more extensive hippocampal damage showed greater semantic memory impairment. Damage extended to adjacent cortex worsened semantic deficits.
Conclusion
The hippocampus is critical for episodic memory and for acquiring new semantic memory. Semantic memory already consolidated before damage may be stored in neocortex independently of the hippocampus.
💡 Link to MSM
Schmolck's findings support the MSM's distinction between STM and LTM: HM's STM was intact but he could not transfer new information to LTM — directly supporting the separate stores proposed by Atkinson & Shiffrin. Use this as AO3 evaluation of the MSM.
Contemporary Study — Reconstructive Memory
Sacchi et al. (2007) — Changing the Past: Misinformation Effects in Personal Autobiographical Memories
+
Aim
To investigate whether personal autobiographical memories can be distorted by post-event misinformation, specifically in the context of political events.
Sample
Approximately 100 Italian adults who had attended a political demonstration in 2001 (Genoa G8 summit protests).
Procedure
Participants were shown a manipulated photograph depicting the protest as more violent (larger crowd, more police presence) or as peaceful. They were then asked to recall their own memories of the event and rate its atmosphere.
Results
Participants shown the manipulated violent image reported remembering the event as more violent and confrontational than those shown the peaceful image or no image — even though they had personally attended. Memory confidence was not reduced despite distortion.
Conclusion
Autobiographical memories are vulnerable to post-event misinformation, supporting a reconstructive model of memory. Even vivid personal memories can be altered by subsequent visual information.
📋 Examiner Report
Sacchi is often confused with Loftus & Palmer. The key distinction: Sacchi used autobiographical memory of a real personal event, not a lab-based video clip. This increases ecological validity but reduces experimental control. Always highlight this difference in evaluation — it's a rich AO3 point.
Methods

Research Methods in Cognitive Psychology

Edexcel requires knowledge of experimental methods, designs, variables, hypotheses, controls, validity, and reliability in the context of cognitive psychology research.

Experimental Methods

Lab Experiment
Controlled environment
Conducted in a controlled artificial setting. High internal validity — IV can be manipulated while extraneous variables are controlled. Low ecological validity. Example: Baddeley's dual-task studies.
Field Experiment
Natural setting
Conducted in a natural environment with IV deliberately manipulated. Higher ecological validity than lab. Less control over extraneous variables. Example: testing memory in real-world contexts.
Natural Experiment
Pre-existing IV
IV occurs naturally; researcher does not manipulate it. High ecological validity. Cannot infer causation — no random allocation. Example: comparing HM's memory to healthy controls.
Quasi-Experiment
Pre-existing groups
Groups based on pre-existing characteristics (e.g. age, gender, amnesiac vs. control). Cannot randomly allocate. Common in neuropsychological memory research.

Experimental Designs

DesignHow it worksStrengthWeakness
Independent GroupsDifferent participants in each condition.No order effects; no demand characteristics from repeated testing.Participant variables — groups may differ; needs more participants.
Repeated MeasuresSame participants in all conditions.Eliminates participant variables; fewer participants needed.Order effects (practice/fatigue); demand characteristics.
Matched PairsParticipants matched on key variables, then split.Controls participant variables; no order effects.Time-consuming to match; impossible to control all variables.

Variables & Hypotheses

Types of Variable
Independent Variable (IV): The variable the researcher manipulates.
Dependent Variable (DV): The variable that is measured.
Extraneous Variable (EV): Any variable other than the IV that may affect the DV.
Confounding Variable: An EV that has actually varied systematically with the IV, threatening internal validity.
Hypotheses
Directional (one-tailed): Predicts the direction of the difference or relationship. Used when prior research strongly suggests an outcome.

Non-directional (two-tailed): Predicts a difference or relationship but not its direction. Used when evidence is limited or mixed.

Validity & Reliability

Validity
Internal validity: Does the study measure what it claims to? Threatened by confounding variables, demand characteristics, social desirability.

External validity: Can findings generalise? Includes ecological validity (real-world applicability) and population validity (generalisability across people).
Reliability
Internal reliability: Consistency within the measure itself (e.g. all items on a test measuring the same thing).

External reliability: Consistency across time and researchers — can the study be replicated with the same results? Assessed via test-retest and inter-rater reliability.
💡 Exam Tip — Validity vs Reliability
A measure can be reliable without being valid (e.g. consistently measuring the wrong thing), but a valid measure must also be reliable. In the exam, always specify which type of validity or reliability you are discussing — generic use of the terms earns minimal marks.

Worked Answers & Examiner Tips

Fully worked sample answers for Cognitive Psychology question types, annotated with AO1/AO2/AO3.

4-Mark — Describe

Working Memory Model4 marks · AO1
"Describe the working memory model." [4 marks]
AO1Central executive: The WMM (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974) proposes that STM is a multi-component active processing system. The central executive is the attentional controller — it allocates resources to the slave systems but has limited capacity and does not store information.
AO1Slave systems: The phonological loop handles verbal/auditory information (split into the phonological store and articulatory process); the visuo-spatial sketchpad processes visual and spatial information.
AO1Episodic buffer: Added by Baddeley (2000), the episodic buffer is a temporary store that integrates information from the slave systems and LTM into a single coherent episode. It has limited capacity and is controlled by the central executive.
AO1 × 3 → 4 marksAll four components named and described with specific functions.
📋 Examiner Report
Students regularly omit the episodic buffer (added 2000) and lose marks. Also common: describing the phonological loop as one unit — the distinction between the phonological store and articulatory process is creditworthy detail.

8-Mark — Outline & Evaluate

Reconstructive Memory8 marks · AO1 + AO3
"Outline and evaluate the concept of reconstructive memory." [8 marks]
AO1Bartlett's theory: Bartlett (1932) proposed that memory is not a passive record but an active reconstructive process. We use schemas — mental frameworks built from prior experience — to fill in gaps and make sense of new information, making memory prone to systematic distortion.
AO1War of the Ghosts: Bartlett demonstrated this using 'The War of the Ghosts' — British participants rationalised culturally unfamiliar elements, omitted schema-inconsistent details, and distorted the story to fit their own knowledge over repeated recalls.
AO3Supporting evidence: Sacchi et al. (2007) found that participants who had personally attended the Genoa G8 protests had their autobiographical memories distorted by a manipulated photograph — supporting the idea that even vivid personal memories are reconstructive and vulnerable to post-event misinformation.
AO3Real-world application: Reconstructive memory theory directly informs policy on eyewitness testimony. If memory is schema-driven, leading questions (as studied by Loftus & Palmer, 1974) can systematically alter what witnesses report — with serious implications for justice.
AO3Methodological criticism: Bartlett's original study lacked standardised procedures and precise participant numbers, making it difficult to replicate or establish cause and effect. The schema concept itself is also difficult to operationalise and test scientifically.
AO1 × 4 + AO3 × 4 = 8 marks2 AO1 paragraphs, 3 developed AO3 points. Band 4.

The most frequently examined themes in Cognitive Psychology are: (1) MSM vs WMM comparison, (2) evaluating reconstructive memory with reference to EWT, and (3) methodology questions about laboratory experiments and validity. Always have a prepared evaluation of both memory models that can be applied flexibly.

Content — CNS & Neurotransmission

The Nervous System & Neurotransmission

The biological approach assumes that behaviour is controlled by biological structures and processes. Understanding the nervous system and how neurons communicate is foundational to the topic.

The Central Nervous System (CNS)
The CNS consists of the brain and spinal cord. It receives sensory information, processes it, and sends motor commands. It is divided into the central (brain + spinal cord) and peripheral nervous systems (somatic + autonomic).
Neuron Type 1
Sensory Neurons
Carry nerve impulses from sense organs toward the CNS. Have long dendrites, short axons. Respond to stimuli such as light, touch, and sound.
Neuron Type 2
Motor Neurons
Carry nerve impulses from the CNS to effectors (muscles and glands). Have short dendrites, long axons. Control voluntary and involuntary movement.
Neuron Type 3
Relay Neurons
Found in the CNS — connect sensory and motor neurons. Process and integrate incoming and outgoing signals. Responsible for reflex arcs.

Synaptic Transmission

When an action potential reaches the axon terminal, it triggers the release of neurotransmitters from synaptic vesicles into the synaptic cleft. These bind to receptor sites on the post-synaptic neuron. If excitatory neurotransmitters cause sufficient depolarisation, a new action potential is fired (excitatory postsynaptic potential, EPSP). Inhibitory neurotransmitters prevent firing (IPSP).

Neurotransmitter 1
Serotonin
Regulates mood, appetite, and sleep. Low serotonin is associated with depression and aggression. SSRIs block its reuptake, increasing availability in the synapse.
Neurotransmitter 2
Dopamine
Associated with reward, motivation, and movement. Excess dopamine activity linked to schizophrenia (dopamine hypothesis). Deficiency linked to Parkinson's disease.
Neurotransmitter 3
Noradrenaline
Involved in the fight-or-flight response. Increases heart rate and alertness. Also plays a role in mood regulation — low levels associated with depression.
Neurotransmitter 4
GABA
The main inhibitory neurotransmitter. Reduces neuronal excitability throughout the nervous system. Dysfunction linked to anxiety disorders. Benzodiazepines work by enhancing GABA activity.
💡 Exam Tip — Excitatory vs Inhibitory
A common exam question asks you to distinguish between excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmitters. Key rule: excitatory NTs make the post-synaptic neuron more likely to fire (depolarise); inhibitory NTs make it less likely (hyperpolarise). Always link to a named neurotransmitter for full marks.
Content — Aggression

Brain Function & Aggression

The biological approach explains aggression through brain structure, neurotransmitter activity, hormones, and genetic factors.

Brain Areas & Aggression

Brain Area 1
Amygdala
Part of the limbic system — involved in processing emotions, especially fear and aggression. Stimulation of the amygdala in animals (Bard & Mountcastle, 1948) produces aggressive responses. Damage may reduce aggression.
Brain Area 2
Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)
Regulates impulse control and decision-making. Damage to the PFC disinhibits aggression — famous case of Phineas Gage. Raine et al. (1997) found reduced PFC activity in murderers.
Brain Area 3
Hypothalamus
Regulates the autonomic nervous system and hormonal responses. Stimulation of certain hypothalamic nuclei triggers aggression. Linked to testosterone and adrenaline release during conflict.

Hormones, Genes & Aggression

Testosterone & Aggression
Testosterone is an androgen hormone produced primarily in the testes (and in smaller amounts in the adrenal glands). High testosterone levels are associated with increased aggression — supported by studies showing higher testosterone in violent offenders. However, the relationship is correlational, and causation cannot be established.

The challenge hypothesis (Wingfield et al., 1990) proposes that testosterone rises in response to social challenges (e.g. competition, perceived threats), priming aggressive responses. This is more nuanced than a simple testosterone → aggression model.

Genetic Explanations
Twin studies suggest a genetic component to aggression. Concordance rates are higher in monozygotic (MZ) twins (~50–80%) than dizygotic (DZ) twins (~10–30%) for aggressive behaviour. The MAOA gene (monoamine oxidase A) has been linked to aggression — the 'warrior gene'. Low MAOA activity leads to excess serotonin, dopamine, and noradrenaline, potentially increasing aggressive responding.
📋 Examiner Report
Students often claim that genetics 'causes' aggression. The correct position is that genes may create a predisposition — not a deterministic outcome. Always acknowledge the gene-environment interaction: the MAOA gene is associated with aggression only when combined with adverse early environments (Caspi et al., 2002). This nuance is essential for high-band AO3.
Content — Biological Rhythms

Circadian & Infradian Rhythms

Biological rhythms are cyclical patterns of physiological and behavioural change regulated by internal biological clocks and external zeitgebers (time-givers).

Rhythm Type 1
Circadian Rhythms
Cycles of approximately 24 hours. Regulated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus — the brain's master clock. Governed by light (main zeitgeber). Examples: sleep-wake cycle, core body temperature, cortisol release.
Rhythm Type 2
Infradian Rhythms
Cycles longer than 24 hours. Examples: the menstrual cycle (~28 days), hibernation, seasonal affective disorder (SAD — annual). Regulated by hormones such as oestrogen and progesterone.
Rhythm Type 3
Ultradian Rhythms
Cycles shorter than 24 hours. Example: the sleep cycle (~90 minutes) alternating between REM and NREM sleep. Also includes basic rest-activity cycle (BRAC) during waking hours.

Endogenous Pacemakers vs Exogenous Zeitgebers

FactorRoleEvidence
SCN (endogenous)Master biological clock — maintains circadian rhythm even without external cues. Receives direct input from the retina via the retinohypothalamic tract.Morgan (1995): transplanting SCN cells with different circadian rhythms into hamsters changed their rhythm.
Melatonin (endogenous)Released by the pineal gland in darkness. Promotes sleep onset. Regulated by the SCN — links light/dark cycle to sleep.Sack et al. (2000): melatonin administration shifts circadian phase — used to treat jet lag.
Light (exogenous)The primary zeitgeber. Light suppresses melatonin, resetting the SCN. Crucial for synchronising internal clock to the 24-hour day.Siffre (1962): without light cues, free-ran to a ~25-hour cycle.
Social cues (exogenous)Meal times, social interaction, and temperature act as secondary zeitgebers that fine-tune circadian rhythms.Aschoff & Wever (1976): isolated participants shifted to ~25-hour cycles without social cues.
Key Studies

Core Studies

All three studies are named in the Edexcel spec. Raine et al. is the classic study; Brendgen and McDermott are the contemporary studies.

Classic Study — Brain & Aggression
Raine et al. (1997) — Brain Abnormalities in Murderers Indicated by Positron Emission Tomography
+
Aim
To investigate whether murderers pleading not guilty by reason of insanity (NGRI) show different brain activity compared to non-murderers using PET scanning.
Sample
41 NGRI murderers (39 male, 2 female) matched with 41 controls on age and sex. 6 NGRI participants had schizophrenia; controls matched accordingly.
Procedure
Participants injected with FDG (radioactive glucose tracer) and completed a continuous performance task (CPT) for 32 minutes. PET scans were taken to measure glucose metabolism in 14 brain areas. Quasi-experimental design — IV was murderer/non-murderer status (pre-existing).
Results
NGRI murderers showed: reduced glucose metabolism in the prefrontal cortex, parietal cortex, and corpus callosum; abnormal asymmetry in the amygdala, thalamus, and hippocampus (left side lower than right). No difference in occipital cortex.
Conclusion
Brain dysfunction in areas associated with impulse control, emotional regulation, and integration of information may predispose some individuals to violent behaviour. However, this does not directly cause murder.

Evaluation

TypePointElaboration
✓ StrengthObjective brain imagingPET scans provide objective, quantitative data on brain activity — avoids observer bias and social desirability issues present in self-report methods.
✓ StrengthWell-matched sampleCareful matching on age, sex, and schizophrenia status strengthens the comparison and reduces confounding variables.
✗ WeaknessCannot establish causationQuasi-experiment — no random allocation. The brain differences may have existed before any violent behaviour; alternatively, prison or medication may have caused them.
✗ WeaknessEthical concernsFindings could be misused to argue for biological determinism — that some people are 'born killers'. Raine himself warns against this interpretation.
✗ WeaknessLimited generalisabilitySample consisted only of NGRI murderers — a highly specific group. Findings cannot generalise to all violent offenders or the general population.
📋 Examiner Report
Students often describe Raine's findings as proving that murderers have abnormal brains that 'cause' violence. This is a deterministic overstatement — always qualify: the brain differences are associated with, not causally responsible for, violent behaviour. The NGRI sample is also crucial context — these are not typical murderers.
Contemporary Study — Genetics & Aggression
Brendgen et al. (2005) — Examining Genetic and Environmental Effects on Social Aggression
+
Aim
To investigate the relative contributions of genetic and environmental factors to two types of aggression: physical aggression and social (relational) aggression.
Sample
234 twin pairs (identical MZ and non-identical DZ), mean age 6 years, drawn from the Quebec Newborn Twin Study. Both same-sex and opposite-sex DZ pairs included.
Procedure
Aggression rated by teachers and peers. Physical aggression: hitting, kicking, biting. Social aggression: excluding peers, spreading rumours, threatening to withdraw friendship. Twin design compared concordance rates between MZ and DZ pairs.
Results
Physical aggression: MZ concordance significantly higher than DZ — strong genetic contribution (~50–60%). Social aggression: DZ concordance was similar to MZ — suggesting shared environment (not genes) was the primary influence.
Conclusion
Physical and social aggression have different aetiologies. Physical aggression has a stronger genetic basis; social aggression is more environmentally determined. This challenges single-cause biological explanations of aggression.
💡 Exam Tip — Using Brendgen
Brendgen is an excellent study to use when evaluating biological explanations of aggression — it shows that the relationship between genes and aggression is not straightforward. Physical and social aggression have different genetic bases, which challenges any single biological theory. Use it as a nuanced AO3 evaluation point.
Contemporary Study — Genes & Aggression
McDermott et al. (2008) — Monoamine Oxidase A Gene (MAOA) Predicts Behavioural Aggression
+
Aim
To investigate whether MAOA gene variants predict aggressive behaviour under conditions of provocation in a laboratory setting.
Sample
Approximately 78 male participants genotyped for MAOA variants (MAOA-L = low activity; MAOA-H = high activity).
Procedure
Participants played a trust game where they could be cheated by a partner (provocation condition) or not cheated (no-provocation control). Aggression was measured by how much hot sauce participants chose to administer to the partner who had cheated them.
Results
MAOA-L participants showed significantly more aggressive behaviour (more hot sauce) than MAOA-H participants in the provocation condition. No significant difference between genotype groups in the no-provocation condition.
Conclusion
The MAOA gene moderates aggressive responses to provocation. MAOA-L confers a predisposition to aggression specifically when provoked — supporting a gene-environment interaction model rather than simple genetic determinism.
📋 Examiner Report
When evaluating McDermott, students often overlook the key finding: MAOA-L only predicted aggression under provocation. This is a crucial nuance — it supports interactionist (not determinist) biological psychology. Also note the 'hot sauce' measure is indirect and arguably lacks mundane realism.
Methods

Research Methods in Biological Psychology

Edexcel requires knowledge of correlational research, brain scanning techniques, twin studies, and Spearman's rank correlation coefficient.

Correlational Research

Core Definition
A correlation investigates the relationship between two co-variables — it measures the direction (positive/negative) and strength of association. Correlations do not establish causation — a third variable may explain the relationship.
Types of Correlation
Positive: Both variables increase together (e.g. testosterone levels and aggression scores).
Negative: One increases as the other decreases (e.g. serotonin levels and depression scores).
Zero: No systematic relationship between variables.
Strengths & Weaknesses
Strengths: Ethical — no manipulation of variables; useful for variables that cannot be experimentally manipulated; identifies relationships for further experimental study.
Weaknesses: Cannot establish causation; directionality problem; third variable problem.
💡 Correlation vs Causation
In the exam, never say a correlation 'proves' or 'shows that X causes Y'. Always use language such as 'there is a positive association between…' or 'X is positively correlated with Y'. This distinction is assessed directly in methodology questions.

Brain Scanning Techniques

Structural
MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)
Uses magnetic fields to produce high-resolution images of brain structure. Non-invasive and safe. Does not measure brain activity — structural only. Used to identify tissue damage and abnormalities.
Functional
fMRI (Functional MRI)
Measures blood oxygenation (BOLD response) as an indirect index of neural activity. High spatial resolution. Reveals which areas are active during tasks. Used to study memory, emotion, and language.
Functional
PET (Positron Emission Tomography)
Measures glucose metabolism — a proxy for neural activity. Requires radioactive tracer injection (FDG). Lower spatial resolution than fMRI. Used by Raine et al. (1997). Invasive procedure.
Electrical
EEG (Electroencephalogram)
Measures electrical activity in the brain via scalp electrodes. Excellent temporal resolution (millisecond level). Poor spatial resolution — cannot localise precisely. Used to study sleep stages and seizures.

Twin Studies

Twin studies exploit the natural experiment of MZ twins (100% genetic overlap) vs DZ twins (~50% genetic overlap) to estimate the heritability of traits. If a trait is more concordant in MZ than DZ twins, this suggests a genetic contribution.

AspectExplanation
Concordance rateThe probability that if one twin has a trait, the other also has it. Expressed as a percentage. MZ > DZ concordance → genetic influence.
HeritabilityEstimated from twin data — the proportion of variance in a trait attributable to genetic factors. Does not mean the trait is fixed; environment still matters.
AssumptionThe equal environments assumption: MZ and DZ twins share equally similar environments. If violated (MZ twins treated more similarly), genetic estimates are inflated.
LimitationMZ twins share 100% of genes but not 100% of prenatal environments. Cannot disentangle all genetic from environmental effects.

Spearman's Rank Correlation

Spearman's rank is a non-parametric statistical test used to measure the strength and direction of a monotonic relationship between two ordinal variables (or non-normally distributed interval data).

When to Use
Use Spearman's when: (1) the hypothesis predicts a correlation/relationship, (2) data is ordinal or ranked, (3) data is not normally distributed. The result is a correlation coefficient (rs) between −1 and +1.
Interpreting Results
Compare calculated rs to the critical value (at appropriate significance level and N). If |calculated| ≥ |critical value| → reject H₀ (result is significant). A result of rs = 0.85 indicates a strong positive correlation.
💡 Exam Tip — Spearman's in Biological Context
A common exam question asks you to calculate or interpret Spearman's in biological psychology — e.g. the correlation between testosterone levels and aggression scores. Always: (1) state the direction and strength of the correlation, (2) compare to the critical value, (3) state whether the result is significant, and (4) link back to the hypothesis.

Worked Answers & Examiner Tips

Fully worked sample answers for Biological Psychology question types, annotated with AO1/AO2/AO3.

4-Mark — Describe

Synaptic Transmission4 marks · AO1
"Describe the process of synaptic transmission." [4 marks]
AO1Action potential: When an action potential reaches the axon terminal (pre-synaptic neuron), it triggers the release of neurotransmitters from synaptic vesicles into the synaptic cleft.
AO1Receptor binding: Neurotransmitters diffuse across the cleft and bind to specific receptor sites on the post-synaptic membrane. The shape of the neurotransmitter must match the receptor (lock-and-key model).
AO1Effect and reuptake: If sufficient excitatory neurotransmitters bind, an EPSP is produced and a new action potential fires in the post-synaptic neuron. Neurotransmitters are then broken down by enzymes or reabsorbed via reuptake for reuse.
AO1 × 3 → 4 marksAction potential, release, binding, effect, and reuptake all mentioned.

8-Mark — Outline & Evaluate

Biological Explanation of Aggression8 marks · AO1 + AO3
"Outline and evaluate one biological explanation of aggression." [8 marks]
AO1Neural explanation: The biological approach proposes that aggression is linked to activity in specific brain structures. The prefrontal cortex regulates impulse control — damage or reduced activity disinhibits aggressive behaviour. The amygdala is involved in emotional processing; stimulation can trigger aggressive responses.
AO1Supporting research: Raine et al. (1997) used PET scans to show that NGRI murderers had significantly reduced glucose metabolism in the prefrontal cortex and abnormal asymmetry in the amygdala and thalamus compared to matched controls — consistent with this neural explanation.
AO3Methodological strength: Raine's use of PET scanning provides objective, physiological data free from social desirability bias — strengthening the validity of the finding that brain activity differs in violent individuals.
AO3Limitation — causation: Raine's study was quasi-experimental with no random allocation — brain differences may be a consequence of violent lifestyle or medication, not a cause of it. This makes it impossible to conclude that neural abnormalities cause aggression.
AO3Alternative evidence: Brendgen et al. (2005) found that social (relational) aggression was more environmentally than genetically determined, while physical aggression showed stronger genetic influence — suggesting that biological explanations apply differently to different types of aggression and cannot account for all aggressive behaviour.
AO1 × 4 + AO3 × 4 = 8 marks2 AO1 paragraphs, 3 developed AO3 points. Band 4.
💡 Biological Psychology — Key Exam Themes
The most frequently examined themes are: (1) neural and hormonal explanations of aggression evaluated with Raine/Brendgen/McDermott, (2) circadian rhythms with reference to the SCN and exogenous zeitgebers, and (3) scanning technique evaluation questions (PET vs fMRI). Always be able to evaluate biological explanations using the key limitation: correlation ≠ causation.
📋 Examiner Report — Biological Psychology Generally
The most common error across biological psychology questions is making deterministic claims — stating that genes or brain structure 'make' people aggressive or violent. The correct position is always probabilistic and interactionist: biology creates a predisposition which interacts with environment. Students who consistently use qualified language ('may predispose', 'associated with', 'increases the likelihood of') consistently score in the higher bands.
